Lesson 1 · The big picture

What is CookSim, and why build it?

The research community uses a small game called overcooked_ai to study how AI agents cooperate. Two cooks share a cramped kitchen and must coordinate to make soup. It's brilliant for research, but it's tiny: one recipe, a handful of tiles, and flat, basic graphics.

CookSim keeps everything that made it useful for AI — a clean, deterministic simulation you can train agents on — and makes it into a real game: many ingredients, genuine multi-step cooking, timed customer orders, a kitchen you can design yourself, and a renderer that actually looks like a kitchen.

Lesson 2 · The world

How a kitchen is built

Every kitchen is a rectangular grid. Each cell is either walkable floor or an impassable feature you use from an adjacent floor tile. Features come in three families:

📦Sources — infinite onions, tomatoes, dough, plates… 🍲Stations — pot, pan, oven, cutting board, sink 🛎️Terminals — the serving pass and the trash bin

Layouts are just text. This compact grammar (one character per tile) is how a kitchen is stored, and it's exactly what the level editor reads and writes:

# the classic "cramped room" — onions, one pot, plates, a pass
XXPXX     X = counter   P = pot   D = plates
O   O     O = onion source
X1 2X     1,2 = where the two cooks spawn
XDXSX     S = serving window
✅ Why a grid? Discrete tiles + discrete actions = a clean, reproducible simulation. Give it the same actions and the same random seed and you get the exact same game every time. That determinism is gold for debugging and for training AI.
Lesson 3 · Cooking

An ingredient has a state: raw → chopped (on a board) or → cooked (in a pot, pan, or oven). Leave it on the heat too long and it burns — now it's worthless and you must bin it. A plate is the workspace: you ladle cooked food onto it, drop chopped pieces on it, add raw items, and when the contents exactly match a recipe, the pass accepts it.

The full menu spans every prep type:

🥣 Onion / Tomato / Mushroom / Mixed Soup 🥗 Garden / Greek Salad · Caprese 🍔 Burger · Cheeseburger · Deluxe Burger 🍳 Fried Fish · Fried Egg · Loaded Fries · Fish & Chips 🍚 Rice Bowl · Sushi · Fried Rice 🍕 Pizza · Veggie Bake

Customers order from this menu on a timer. Each order ticket shows the recipe and its steps, and a bar counts down — fill it in time for the reward, or eat a penalty when it expires.

💡 One elegant trick. Cooking stations are recipe-blind — a pot just turns whatever is inside from raw to cooked, then to burnt. Nothing in the kitchen "knows" about pizza. A recipe is only checked at the very end, at the serving pass, by comparing the plate's exact contents to the menu. That keeps the simulation tiny while allowing any combination a player can physically assemble.
Lesson 4 · The simulation

The engine: one tick at a time

The whole world advances in discrete ticks. Each tick, every cook submits one action, and the engine resolves them in a fixed order so the result is always deterministic:

  1. Move & face. Movement is resolved together so two cooks can't walk into the same tile or swap places — collisions cancel cleanly.
  2. Interact. Each cook acting on INTERACT affects the tile it faces — pick up, put down, add to a pot, chop, wash, or serve. The same action, context-dependent.
  3. Tick the world. Pots cook a step closer (or burn), and order timers count down. Expired orders apply a penalty.

Driving the simulator directly is just a loop:

from cooksim import KitchenGame, load_layout
from cooksim.agents import GreedyChef

game = KitchenGame(load_layout("pizzeria"), seed=0)
bots = [GreedyChef(i) for i in range(game.n_players)]

while not game.done:
    actions = [bot(game, i) for i, bot in enumerate(bots)]
    game.step(actions)          # advance one tick

print(game.score, dict(game.stats))   # e.g. 1086.3  {'deliveries': 49}
🧩 Separation of concerns. The engine knows nothing about graphics or AI. It just owns the rules and emits a plain data snapshot of the world each tick. The renderer, the network server, and the RL trainers are all thin shells around that one snapshot — so each can change without touching the others.
Lesson 5 · Teaching machines to cook

Reinforcement-learning environments

Because the engine is clean and deterministic, it makes an excellent training ground for AI. CookSim ships two standard interfaces researchers already use:

  • PettingZoo (CookSimParallelEnv) — every cook is an agent acting at once. This is the canonical multi-agent, fully-cooperative setting.
  • Gymnasium (CookSim-v0) — control one cook against a fixed partner, the classic single-agent setup.
from cooksim.envs import CookSimParallelEnv

env = CookSimParallelEnv(layout="open_kitchen", reward_mode="shared")
obs, info = env.reset(seed=0)
while env.agents:
    actions = {a: env.action_space(a).sample() for a in env.agents}
    obs, rewards, term, trunc, info = env.step(actions)

What does an agent actually see? Not pixels — a compact stack of feature planes laid over the grid: where I am, where my teammates are, what's on every counter, how full and how cooked each pot is, plus the order queue and the clock. It's everything you'd need to reason about the kitchen, in a form a neural network can read.

✅ Reward shaping. Delivering a dish is the real reward, but it's rare — so the env can hand out small bonuses for useful sub-actions (chop something, fill a pot, plate a soup). That dense feedback is what makes a fresh agent start to learn instead of flailing. You can switch it off for pure evaluation.
Lesson 6 · Making it look real

A real-time 3D renderer

This is where CookSim leaves the original far behind. The kitchen is a genuine 3D scene, rendered live in your browser with WebGL via Three.js. Counters and walls are real extruded geometry, chefs are 3D characters, and the food are little 3D models — all lit by a virtual studio:

☀️ A key light casting soft real-time shadows (shadow maps) 🪞 Image-based lighting — steel pots & pans reflect the room 🎞️ ACES film tone-mapping for a cinematic colour response 🌟 Bloom post-processing makes the oven & sparks glow 🪵 Procedural wood & tile textures ♨️ Steam, fire and sparkle particles; smooth chef motion

Remarkably, there are still no downloaded art assets. Every pot, onion, chef and tile is built from primitive shapes and procedural textures in code — so the whole game is self-contained, yet looks like a real kitchen from any angle.

💡 Why it suddenly looks real. Three ingredients do most of the work: shadow maps (so objects sit on the floor instead of floating), image-based lighting (so metal reflects something instead of looking like flat grey paint), and tone mapping (which compresses bright highlights the way a real camera does). Add a touch of bloom and the kitchen comes alive.
Lesson 7 · The built-in chef

How the AI chef thinks

So you're never cooking alone, CookSim includes a hand-written "GreedyChef." It's not a trained network — it's a small, readable decision-maker that plans like a person would:

  1. Pick a dish. Choose an order it can actually finish in time, and commit — no flip-flopping mid-cook. When there are several cooks, they deliberately pick different dishes so they don't trip over each other.
  2. Produce the parts. Carry raw items to the right station — onions to a pot, a patty to a pan, lettuce to a board — and stand and chop where chopping is needed.
  3. Assemble & serve. Grab a clean plate, ladle on the cooked food, add the chopped and raw bits, and walk it to the pass.

Navigation is a breadth-first search across floor tiles to the nearest spot beside the target station; a small anti-stuck rule breaks the rare deadlock when two cooks want the same tile.

🧠 Honest limits. GreedyChef is a baseline, not a genius. Solo, it's strong — dozens of deliveries a round. With several cooks in a tight kitchen it can still bump into itself, and the "Forced Coordination" map is deliberately unsolvable without true teamwork. That gap is exactly the kind of problem the RL environments above exist to study.
Lesson 8 · Make your own

The visual level editor

You don't have to write that tile-grammar by hand. The browser editor lets you paint a kitchen, drop in spawn points, resize the grid, and validate that it's actually playable (it checks you have a pass, plates, and ingredient sources). It can also procedurally generate a fresh kitchen, and import/export layouts as JSON.

Hit ▶ Play this layout and your kitchen loads straight into the game — handed off through the browser's local storage, no save files required.

Lesson 9 · A real game loop

Pick a kitchen, earn your stars

CookSim wraps all of this in an actual game: a level-select screen, a co-op mode (two cooks on one keyboard, or fill empty seats with bots), a countdown round, and a star rating when service ends.

Controls. Cook 1: arrow keys to move, Space to use. Cook 2: WASD + F. Hold interact on a board to chop, on a sink to wash. Pots cook automatically when full — scoop with a clean plate, then deliver at the pass.

Run it yourself

# create the environment and install
conda env create -f environment.yml
conda activate cooksim

# launch the game + editor server, then open http://127.0.0.1:8000
cooksim-server

# or just use the simulator for AI research
python examples/greedy_demo.py
pytest -q          # 20 tests: mechanics, RL conformance, determinism
Recap

What you just learned

CookSim is one clean idea — a grid where a single interact action does everything — scaled into a complete game:

  • A deterministic engine with multi-step cooking, burning, washing and timed orders.
  • Two standard RL environments (Gymnasium + PettingZoo) with grid-feature observations and reward shaping.
  • A self-contained real-time 3D renderer (Three.js) with shadows, reflections, bloom and particles — no art assets.
  • A level editor, procedural maps, a planning AI chef, and a menu→play→stars game loop.
